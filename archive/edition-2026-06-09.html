Ctruh
Deep Tech · $2.50M · Seed · Bengaluru · Inflection Point Ventures
The Setup
Ctruh builds a browser-native 3D and extended reality infrastructure that lets brands deploy immersive product experiences across devices without downloads, pairing it with VersaAI, a tool that converts images and video into 3D assets.
The Play
An infrastructural play, betting on the 3D/XR (extended reality, basically VR and various forms of three-dimensional graphics) space. The assumption is that 3D commerce is approaching its Shopify moment, meaning they are aiming to become so ubiquitous that every mid-market brand can use their product without specialised engineers or hardware, thanks to a simplified UI. The syndicated round with Inflection Point, Avinya, and various smaller participants implies conviction across early-stage India funds, which makes sense to me considering Ctruh seem to be the dominant player in the, granted, early XR space. They have mentioned expanding into the US and UAE, which raises a few questions, the primary one being whether they would be better off consolidating the Indian market first.
The Nuance
The genuinely compelling part of Ctruh's position is the no-code, browser-native architecture, because if 3D experiences require neither a download nor a developer, the TAM expands from enterprise budgets to any brand with a Shopify store. The risk is that the same technical progress making this possible for Ctruh is also making it possible for Adobe, Canva, and Shopify itself to add comparable features natively, and a $2.5M seed against that competition is a very thin cushion. Which brings me back to my initial thought: they may be picking unnecessary battles when there is a lot left on the table in their home market.
The Bet: Watching...
Sahi
Fintech - Trading Platform · $33.00M · Series B · Bengaluru · Accel
The Setup
Sahi is a mobile-first trading platform offering professional-grade tools for individual traders, covering stocks and futures and options through a single-screen interface with real-time analytics built around speed and simplicity.
The Play
Accel continuing from Series A to lead this Series B implies confidence. The key is to focus more on user growth and activity rather than trading volumes or other financial metrics, which tend to be cyclical. The 19x growth in active traders in FY26 is no joke, even despite SEBI's F&O tightening: SEBI changed various rules regarding trading futures and options, which could put customers off from trading.
The Nuance
I am usually a bit wary of regulatory-sensitive sectors, but the founding team is some consolation here: Dale Vaz built Swiggy's engineering at serious scale and Manish Jain from Kotak Securities brings financial distribution instincts, covering the "fin" and the "tech." I do see a lot of upside for fintech companies over the long run considering India's growing middle class and slow but steady rising financial awareness. A key observation is that they are also pivoting into wealth management tech, which seems to be the theme across the fintech sector, with the likes of Groww, Zerodha, and Kuvera also trying to be all-in-one super finance apps, but I do not have an issue with it as long as they can get the "tech" of fintech right.
The Bet: IN!
Snabbit
Quick Home Services · $56.00M · Series D · Mumbai · Susquehanna Venture Capital
The Setup
Snabbit is an on-demand domestic help platform that dispatches trained and background-checked workers for cleaning, dishwashing, and cooking within 10 to 15 minutes, operating across Delhi NCR, Mumbai, and Bengaluru.
The Play
A two-year-old startup raising $112M in total presents quite an interesting case. I am really getting tired of the 10-minute gimmick, but what is more intriguing is the micromarket density model underneath it: the idea that concentrating supply within tight neighbourhoods of major metros changes the unit economics of domestic labour at scale in a way that a city-wide dispatch model never could.
The Nuance
The founder's Zepto lineage is the clearest signal of what Snabbit is actually building, the quick commerce playbook applied to labour rather than groceries, and that framing is genuinely interesting because domestic help is higher frequency and stickier than a grocery order. Speaking anecdotally (my mother's frustration with the morning help), the issue with household labour seems to be quality over quantity. There is no shortage of supply, but where Snabbit can differentiate is through prior training and background checking. Two major concerns: first, can Snabbit really deliver improved quality and service to the point that it warrants a hefty margin over a full-time, highly affordable alternative? Second, worker retention. Trained, background-checked domestic workers who build their own client relationships through the platform have every incentive to eventually go off-platform. How does Snabbit retain them?
The Bet: Unconvinced.
The Week
Three very different products, three very different markets, but each one is essentially a bet that Indian consumers and businesses are ready for more sophisticated experiences, though each faces its own sectoral hurdles.
