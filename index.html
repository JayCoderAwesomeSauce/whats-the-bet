Speedioo
Used two-wheeler startup · ₹10 Cr · Seed · Pune · Atomic Capital
The Setup
Speedioo is a full-stack B2B2C platform for buying and selling used two-wheelers in India, handling sourcing, inspection, pricing, paperwork, and dealer distribution. It operates across Bengaluru, Mumbai, and Pune through a network of over 200 dealer partners, with a franchise-led expansion model targeting Tier 1 through Tier 4 cities.
The Play
Quite an opportunity here: the used two-wheeler market in India is one of those sectors that is obviously large, with roughly 200 million two-wheelers on Indian roads, and the resale market is deeply fragmented and trust-deficient. I have no doubt there is a large market to capture; what raises questions is why hasn't this market been captured already, and the most apparent reason seems to be that this market just has too many moving parts. The chief issue to me is just the information asymmetry: sellers will always have more knowledge than buyers,how can Speedioo ensure quality control and honest sales at scale? Another headache is the lack of standardization: different cities and varying levels of usage make for tough price discovery. A lot of ventures targeting the 2nd/3rd India like to sell hopes of expansion into Tier 2/3/4 cities due to expansion of TAM, but I think in most cases they are in fact much harder than a Bangalore or a Gurgaon. They have to build trust and scale, both at much thinner margins.
The Nuance
If there is one thing to commend Speedioo for, and this is quite a remarkable feat, it's building a cash flow positive business, at this stage, in such a logistically hard space. 30 Cr in GMV, 4000+ vehicles sold, 5x topline growth over the last year. Another aspect to keep in mind is the founding team: both Sagar Potphode and Ajit Deshmukh come from CredR and Rentomojo, which means they have already seen what breaks in used two-wheeler transactions at scale and, ideally, know how to build around it. Given such a similar background, which frankly speaking didn't scale to the heights the initial ambitions hoped for, one has to ask what is different this time? Simply put: EVs. Given the advent of EVs in India, Speedioo unlocks a sizable population which will need a place to offload their current vehicle in exchange for a new EV purchase. Another question would be how they tackle second-hand EVs. Charging infrastructure bottlenecks already cross out the majority of Tier 2+ cities, along with various nuances that come with EVs such as battery degradation and model obsolescence (with rapidly advancing technology, a two-year-old EV may feel significantly outdated). All questions being asked, Speedioo is somewhat well positioned given their partnerships with large EV OEMs such as Ather, Ola, etc., and I am optimistic about their ability to seize the market.
The Bet: IN!
Sarvam
AI · $234M · Series B · Bengaluru · HCLTech
The Setup
Sarvam builds full-stack AI infrastructure for Indian languages, offering speech-to-text, text-to-speech, translation, and conversational agents across 22 languages, alongside its own foundation models trained from scratch in India.
The Play
Sarvam's core bet is that sovereign AI infrastructure (models trained on Indian soil) with government and defence deployments already locked in, becomes structurally impossible to dislodge once institutional adoption reaches critical mass. It sells to enterprises and governments and processes over 10 million API calls daily. Reaching 17 million farmers and 45 million insurance customers through government-led programs creates a distribution footprint no purely commercial competitor can buy quickly. Adding to their moat is their lead investor: HCLTech, a services giant that sells technology delivery to large enterprises globally, taking a 10.46% strategic stake. This is an infrastructural play, and implies HCLTech's faith in future enterprise demand for native AI services. I think there is a case to be made for India's only foundation model trained from scratch, but a 1.5B valuation at a 5M revenue is quite a bold move by HCLTech, and it will be interesting to see if Sarvam can justify it.
The Nuance
Pratyush Kumar's research pedigree, an ETH Zurich PhD layered over IIT Bombay and stints at Microsoft Research and IBM Research, gives the technical foundation credibility in a sector where that distinction matters, but it takes a lot more to establish an AI lab from scratch. In May of 2025, Sarvam released Sarvam-M, a 24-billion-parameter model they positioned as a significant step in their foundational model roadmap. Upon scrutiny, it was realized that the model wasn't trained from scratch, but rather fine-tuning a French open-source model called Mistral. Since then, they have released Sarvam 105B and Sarvam 30B, which have in fact been trained from scratch thanks to government-provided compute and a well-funded team, but it begs the question: can Sarvam justify their price point when enterprises could simply fine-tune open-source models to their needs, paying heed to Llama having developed Hindi as a supported language? In terms of scale and technology, I think Sarvam is peerless with regards to the Indian landscape, but that doesn't mean global hyperscalers aren't up for a challenge.
The Bet: Watching...
SolarSquare
Solar · $53M · Series C · Mumbai · B Capital
The Setup
SolarSquare provides end-to-end rooftop solar solutions for Indian homeowners, covering consultation, design, installation, financing, and maintenance across 29 cities.
The Play
The most interesting number in SolarSquare's announcement is not the $53 million but the category inflection Shreya Mishra describes: from one lakh homes adopting rooftop solar per year five years ago to one lakh every ten days today. Cumulative installed solar capacity surged from about 3 GW in 2014 to over 150 GW in 2026, a 50x increase in 12 years. One of the most robust setups I've seen, SolarSquare's most defensible asset is not its brand but the data it accumulates by owning the entire customer relationship from consultation through years of maintenance. A vertically integrated model that includes financing, performance guarantees, and energy management creates switching costs that a pure installation player cannot replicate, and over 50,000 monitored residential systems represents a proprietary dataset on Indian home energy consumption that grows more valuable as the company moves into battery storage.
The Nuance
Functioning in a highly fragmented market, their only formidable competitor is Tata Power Solar. Granted it will be hard to match them on scale, I think if SolarSquare can play their cards right they can mark off a sizable chunk of the market for themselves. The PM Surya Ghar Muft Bijli Yojana, launched 2024, provides subsidies covering up to 60% of rooftop solar installation costs for households, paired with the DCR mandate as of June 1st, 2026, which states all solar projects must use solar panels that are manufactured in India: this provides an almost too-good-to-be-true setup for SolarSquare. Having various partnerships with Indian manufacturers, and an established residential pivot from initial enterprise targets (residential projects are much higher margin than enterprise), I think the macro-factors themselves warrant quite a confident bet.
The Bet: IN!
The Week
A theme of India's push toward technological self-sufficiency, crystallised by the government's push with various incentive acts, is reshaping where private capital wants to sit. Excited to see more infrastructural bets and technological advancements after an era of quick commerce and AI wrappers.

The Framework
Every startup is analyzed through a 4-legged framework. I don't want to quantify each metric with a rating, as I feel that could make the process too rigid, but I will roughly go over these 4 parameters in each of my analyses.
Founder: Does the team have the domain knowledge, execution history, and personal credibility to pull this off?
Moat: Looking for structural advantages: network effects, switching costs, proprietary data/technology, or distribution that's hard to replicate.
Timing & Market: Is the world set up for this to win right now? This covers market size, regulatory conditions, and whether the problem is urgent enough for people to actually change their behaviour.
Commercial Clarity: How does the business hold up at scale? Is there a clear path to positive margins?
The weightage of each metric varies with each case, for example, a pre-seed deep-tech company would draw more weightage to its moat or founder's expertise, whereas a quick-commerce unicorn raising a Series D would be evaluated more strictly on macro factors and their strategy going forward.
