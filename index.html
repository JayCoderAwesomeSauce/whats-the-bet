The Land Bankers
Proptech · 0.848 · Seed · Bhubaneshwar · Rainmatter
The Setup
The Land Bankers is an AI-assisted land due diligence platform that aggregates land records, runs title verification and dispute checks, and provides ongoing property monitoring. Currently operating in Odisha, it targets a market where unclear land ownership is one of India's most persistent and underserved legal problems.
The Play
Land title disputes account for a disproportionate share of civil litigation in India, and yet the infrastructure for verifying ownership before a transaction has remained almost entirely offline and opaque. One thing that gets me riled up is the Indian startup space's tendency to cluster around the same few convenient ideas, all the while neglecting various, much more severe issues. Every week we are presented a new AI coding assistant which can also do your laundry and take care of your parents, meanwhile the kirana stores down the road are being flooded with notebooks full of accounts receivable. I seem to be in agreement with Rainmatter, which is precisely the gap they are betting on here. Rainmatter's thesis has always leaned toward the digitisation of processes that most Indians navigate through intermediaries and informal networks, and land records fit that template almost perfectly. Land disputes are a problem quite unique to India, at least in the way we go about them. In case the family feud isn't enough, land dispute in India comes with various other complications such as Benami transactions, multiple overlapping systems, and poor digitisation. Land is the primary asset for most Indian families — it's a wealth store, it's a collateral for a loan, and most infamously a basis of inheritance. Besides the millions of families, land dispute is also a major pain point for the real estate and manufacturing industry, causing a lot of friction in logistics, setting up factories, etc.
The Nuance
Amarendra Sahu's founder profile is the most complex variable here. He co-built NestAway into one of India's larger rental platforms, which establishes proptech execution credibility, but his exit involved an FIR against co-founders and institutional investors including Tiger Global and Goldman Sachs. A founder with a chip on his shoulder and experience with institutional dispute makes for a promising narrative, but I'm intrigued by how this business model plays out. It seems pretty apparent that this will be targeting majorly tier 2 and 3 India, in a low-frequency, high-trust kind of sector. Civilian disputes seem more of a transactional, one-time service, but what seems exciting is a B2B expansion. Large builders and manufacturers could do away with the land dispute headache, also providing a recurring source of revenue to TLB. Quite interesting that this is based in and starting out of Odisha, which has some of India's most advanced digital land records, as well as a plethora of land disputes driven by their mining and industrial development due to rich ore concentration. All in all, I'd say it's a very interesting idea tackling an issue that deserves acknowledgement.
The Bet: IN!
Mitigata
Cyber Risk And Insurance · 15 · Series B · Bengaluru · Bessemer Venture Partners
The Setup
Mitigata is a full-stack cyber resilience platform that combines AI-powered security operations, compliance automation, and cyber insurance brokerage into a single product layer. Founded in 2023, it serves over 800 organisations across BFSI, healthcare, and manufacturing, and claims to be India's first IRDAI-regulated broker focused exclusively on cyber insurance.
The Play
Another fascinating business here, providing both security and insurance to cover in cases of breaching that security. Bessemer's bet here is really a bet on regulatory inevitability, with the enforcement of India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act (DPDPA). With the advent of this act, companies could have to pay up to ₹250 crore for a single breach, and each breach stacks up — a single data breach could theoretically trigger the ₹250 crore penalty for inadequate security and the ₹200 crore penalty for failure to notify simultaneously. A single enforcement action could theoretically result in cumulative fines exceeding ₹650 crore, meaning companies can no longer treat cybersecurity as an afterthought. An upside to Mitigata's model is the position to play both sides: the insurer has every incentive to improve the policyholder's security posture, and the security operator has every incentive to accurately price the risk. Offering insurance on their own services speaks to confidence about their own work, which is always appreciated.
The Nuance
Ransomware activity up 38% year-on-year across the Indian mid-market, a 12-month DPDP compliance window, and softening cyber insurance renewal terms all converge to create the perfect macro conditions for Mitigata. Tackling the moat question: their primary underlying capability — AI-assisted threat detection, risk scoring, compliance automation — is something that global players like CrowdStrike and Palo Alto are also building at significantly greater scale and with significantly deeper training data. Mitigata's defensibility lives in its IRDAI broker licence and rootedness in India's regulatory environment. Curious to see how they can scale given their India-specific moat doesn't hold in international markets against the likes of Palo Alto, but their data-driven flywheel that is Gordon AI could provide formidable competition.
The Bet: IN!
CRED
Fintech · 900 · Series H · Bengaluru · Meta
The Setup
CRED is a members-only financial platform gating access to credit card bill payments, personal loans, rent payments, and lifestyle rewards behind a minimum credit score threshold, monetising its high-trust, high-income user base through lending products, merchant partnerships, and financial services distribution.
The Play
I don't think I went 2 LinkedIn posts without the mention of Kunal Shah over the past week. With Kunal Shah joining Meta being the talk of the town, CRED raising $900M seems to be going under the radar. $900M for 20% values them at $4.5B, which is a meaningful dip from their peak at $6.4B in 2022. Granted this investment is also part talent acquisition, as Meta likes to do (Alexandr Wang — Scale AI), there are questions to be asked about what CRED's road ahead looks like. The open question is whether CRED's institutional momentum survives its architect's departure well enough to reach an IPO under an interim CEO who has never run the company before.
The Nuance
CRED's founding logic has always been that the top 2 to 3 percent of India's credit-worthy population is a sufficiently large and sufficiently profitable cohort to build a durable financial services business around. Has that thesis been justified? It's complicated... After 7 years of operations, CRED reported net losses of ₹1,457 crore; the caveat is that only ₹298 crore of that is operating losses. What's interesting is CRED is at near breakeven at a revenue of ₹2,735 crore; the large loss figure is almost entirely the consequence of granting ESOPs at the peak of the ZIRP era. With operating losses down 51%, revenue up 16%, and a 34% increase in transaction frequency, all in FY25, there is no denying the business model works. The only thing to keep in mind is that this was built by Kunal Shah, a proven consumer mastermind. How the markets will evaluate a founder-dependent fintech without their founder, heading into an IPO, seems somewhat dubious.
The Bet: Watching...
The Week
India's compliance and data infrastructure is being forced into the open simultaneously: the DPDPA's enforcement window and SEBI's coupled with the government's push to digitise land records will lead to disruption, but these are necessary steps towards becoming a developed society.

The Framework
Every startup is analyzed through a 4-legged framework. I don't want to quantify each metric with a rating, as I feel that could make the process too rigid, but I will roughly go over these 4 parameters in each of my analyses.
Founder: Does the team have the domain knowledge, execution history, and personal credibility to pull this off?
Moat: Looking for structural advantages: network effects, switching costs, proprietary data/technology, or distribution that's hard to replicate.
Timing & Market: Is the world set up for this to win right now? This covers market size, regulatory conditions, and whether the problem is urgent enough for people to actually change their behaviour.
Commercial Clarity: How does the business hold up at scale? Is there a clear path to positive margins?
The weightage of each metric varies with each case, for example, a pre-seed deep-tech company would draw more weightage to its moat or founder's expertise, whereas a quick-commerce unicorn raising a Series D would be evaluated more strictly on macro factors and their strategy going forward.
